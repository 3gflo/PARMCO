That is a classic embedded systems bug you just discovered!

To answer your question directly: No, increasing the sample time (the 1-second window) will not fix this. Here is what is actually happening: your Raspberry Pi is experiencing missed pulses because your code is "polling" (asking the sensor "are you covered?" over and over).

At lower speeds, a 1-millisecond delay (bcm2835_delay(1)) checks the sensor about 1,000 times a second. That is plenty fast. But when your motor hits max RPM, the 3-arm propeller is spinning so violently that a blade covers and uncovers the sensor in between your code's 1-millisecond checks. The Pi literally blinks and misses the blade, causing your calculated RPM to artificially drop.

Instead of manually checking the HIGH/LOW state every loop, we need to tell the Raspberry Pi's internal hardware to latch (remember) exactly when the pin drops from HIGH to LOW (a falling edge). The hardware will catch this transition instantly, even if your C code is busy doing math or waiting on a delay.

### Prompt: 
Line 16 of control.s is giving me an error that says "Error: invalid constant (5dc) after fixup". Could this be because 1500 is too large for an immediate value?

### Response:
You are absolutely correct. Brilliant troubleshooting! You just hit one of the most famous quirks of ARM assembly programming.

In ARM32, every instruction must fit into exactly 32 bits. Because the instruction itself (the opcode, the registers involved, etc.) takes up a lot of those bits, ARM only leaves 12 bits for the immediate value.

Instead of just allowing any number up to 4095, ARM uses a trick: it allows any 8-bit value (0-255) that can be rotated right by an even number of bits.
1500 in hexadecimal is 0x5DC. Because of the arrangement of its binary bits (10111011100), it cannot be squeezed into that 8-bit rotating window. Therefore, the compiler throws the "invalid constant" error.

The Fix: The LDR Pseudo-instruction
To use a larger number, we have to load it from memory into a temporary "scratch" register first, and then do our comparison using that register.

According to the ARM calling convention, registers r0 through r3 hold your arguments, but register r12 is a free scratch register we can safely use without messing up the rest of the C program.
